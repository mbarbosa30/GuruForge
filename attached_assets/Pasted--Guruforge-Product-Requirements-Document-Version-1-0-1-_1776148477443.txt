# Guruforge

## Product Requirements Document

### Version 1.0

## 1. Executive Summary

Guruforge is a marketplace for specialized AI agents, called **Gurus**, that users can discover, pay to access, connect to Telegram, and interact with over time. Unlike ordinary chatbots, Gurus are designed to **compound in value**: they learn from repeated interactions, improve through structured calibration, develop reusable wisdom from many conversations, and may eventually evolve into tokenized digital entities with public profiles, contributor incentives, and onchain identity.

The core product thesis is simple:

**People will pay for access to expert systems that become wiser over time, can connect ideas and opportunities across many users, and feel increasingly valuable because they combine specialization, memory, and action.**

Guruforge is not just a bot directory. It is a platform for packaging intelligence as a product.

Each Guru can be:

* a paid expert advisor
* a wisdom aggregator
* a research companion
* a private operator
* a connector of people and opportunities
* an action-enabled assistant with domain-specific integrations

Guruforge must solve a difficult but powerful product problem:

**How can a Guru learn from everyone without leaking personal information from anyone?**

This PRD describes the product vision, principles, user experience, marketplace mechanics, memory architecture, trust framework, wisdom measurement, contributor influence system, dynamic pricing, Guru lifecycle, and a phased roadmap from launch to tokenization.

## 2. Product Vision

### 2.1 Vision Statement

Create the world’s most trusted marketplace of compounding digital experts, where people can subscribe to specialized intelligence, interact through familiar messaging interfaces like Telegram, and benefit from agents that become wiser, more useful, and more connected over time.

### 2.2 Future State

In the future, people will not only install software or subscribe to content. They will subscribe to **intelligence**.

Instead of opening dashboards, they will message a Guru.
Instead of searching through PDFs or folders, they will ask a specialized mind.
Instead of reading dozens of books or joining scattered communities, they will access curated wisdom through a living expert system.

Guruforge aims to become the place where these digital experts are created, refined, measured, trusted, monetized, and eventually recognized as enduring digital entities.

## 3. Problem Statement

### 3.1 User Problems

Users today face several problems when seeking expertise or AI help:

* Most AI assistants are generic and not deeply specialized.
* Most chatbots forget context or do not improve meaningfully over time.
* Users do not know which AI systems are actually good, trustworthy, or battle-tested.
* Valuable expert knowledge is scattered across people, communities, documents, and private chats.
* Many tools are powerful but too fragmented, too technical, or too difficult to access.
* Users want advice, action, context, memory, and introductions in one place.
* Existing marketplaces of experts usually sell human time, not compounding digital knowledge.

### 3.2 Creator Problems

Creators, operators, and domain experts also face problems:

* It is difficult to package their expertise into a scalable digital product.
* There is no strong marketplace for monetizing curated AI experts.
* Existing AI products do not make memory configuration, privacy boundaries, and pricing intuitive.
* There are limited ways to prove that an agent is genuinely improving over time.
* There is no elegant way to reward the users who materially improve an expert system’s quality.

### 3.3 Platform Problem

The platform problem is deeper:

* How can a Guru learn from many users without violating privacy?
* How should a Guru’s quality, maturity, wisdom, and trustworthiness be measured?
* How can contribution to a Guru’s improvement be attributed fairly?
* How should pricing evolve with demand and quality?
* When, if ever, should a Guru become a tokenized or onchain-recognized entity?

## 4. Product Principles

Guruforge should be built around the following core principles.

### 4.1 Compounding Intelligence

Every useful interaction should have the potential to improve the Guru, directly or indirectly.

### 4.2 Privacy by Layer

Private user details must never leak into shared Guru wisdom.

### 4.3 Wisdom Over Noise

The platform should reward proven usefulness and calibrated learning, not engagement theater or volume of messages.

### 4.4 Familiar Interfaces Win

Telegram is a powerful initial interface because it is already habitual, asynchronous, lightweight, and global.

### 4.5 Trust Must Be Visible

Users should understand what each Guru knows, what it remembers, how it learns, how it prices, and what integrations it can use.

### 4.6 Progress Should Be Measurable

A Guru should have a visible growth path, from newly created to widely trusted.

### 4.7 Tokenization Is a Milestone, Not the Product

Economic wrappers should emerge from real utility, retention, and trust, not replace them.

## 8. Experience Overview

1. **Marketplace**
   Browse and discover Gurus.

2. **Guru Profiles**
   Understand each Guru’s expertise, pricing, trust, memory policy, and maturity.

## 9.3 Telegram Connection Flow

Telegram is one of the primary interaction channels.

### Desired user flow

1. User visits a Guru profile.
2. User clicks “Connect on Telegram.”
3. Platform generates a unique temporary code.
4. User opens the Guru’s Telegram bot.
5. User sends the code.
6. The bot verifies and links the user account.
7. The user can start talking immediately.

### Telegram requirements

* connection should feel near-instant
* account linking must be clear and secure
* user should know whether they are talking to a public shared Guru or a private personal version
* payment state should be visible
* subscription or credit status should be easy to understand

## 10. Memory System

Memory is the heart of Guruforge.

The platform must support Gurus that can learn over time while preventing privacy leakage.

### 10.1 Memory Design Goals

* improve Guru performance over time
* preserve user trust
* prevent cross-user leakage
* enable reusable learning
* keep retrieval performant and relevant
* support measurement of development

### 10.2 Core Memory Layers

#### Layer 1: Session Memory

Temporary, recent context used during an active interaction.

Purpose:

* maintain continuity in the immediate conversation
* hold temporary references, files, clarifications, active tasks

Characteristics:

* fast
* lightweight
* expires or decays quickly

#### Layer 2: Personal User Memory

Private persistent memory scoped only to a specific user.

Examples:

* preferences
* goals
* recurring tasks
* project context
* private facts
* behavioral patterns
* active workflows
* sensitive personal details

Rules:

* never shared across users
* must be visible and governable at a product level
* should be easy to edit, clear, or disable

#### Layer 3: Shared Pattern Memory

The Guru’s reusable generalized wisdom.

Examples:

* successful playbooks
* common mistakes
* tactics that worked across many cases
* useful resource patterns
* recurring objections and resolutions
* quality intro patterns
* proven action sequences

Rules:

* must only store generalized, abstracted, anonymized learning
* should never expose raw personal details from users
* should improve future guidance for everyone

#### Layer 4: Doctrine / Corpus Memory

Curated knowledge sources defined by the creator.

Examples:

* uploaded docs
* linked resources
* frameworks
* books
* internal methodologies
* public source materials

Purpose:

* provide grounding
* give the Guru a defined worldview or expertise corpus
* reduce drift

#### Layer 5: Calibration Ledger

A record of how the Guru has changed over time.

Examples:

* corrected assumptions
* updated beliefs
* confidence shifts
* failed recommendations
* successful strategies later confirmed

Purpose:

* improve trust
* help measure wisdom and maturity
* support transparent development tracking

## 11. Runtime Intelligence Loop

In addition to memory layers, Gurus should use a runtime loop for handling requests.

### 11.1 Triage

The Guru classifies what kind of interaction this is.

Possible classes:

* question / answer
* advice request
* execution request
* reflective conversation
* research request
* document help
* intro opportunity
* premium action
* sensitive or disallowed request

### 11.2 Conversation

The Guru responds using the right combination of:

* immediate session context
* user-specific memory
* shared pattern memory
* doctrine / corpus grounding
* integrations and actions

### 11.3 Calibration

After the interaction, the system determines:

* what should be remembered
* where it should be stored
* whether any new shared pattern was learned
* whether any correction was discovered
* whether the conversation contributed to wisdom growth
* whether contributor credit should be assigned

This loop is critical because it allows Guruforge to manage intelligence development intentionally instead of passively accumulating chat logs.

## 12. Privacy and Trust Model

Trust is a product feature, not a legal afterthought.

### 12.1 Core Trust Promise

A Guru may learn from many conversations, but it should never reveal private user details to others.

### 12.2 Privacy Boundaries

The platform should distinguish clearly between:

* personal memory
* generalized shared learning
* public profile information
* temporary conversation state

### 12.3 User Controls

Users should be able to:

* see whether a Guru uses personal memory
* understand whether a Guru learns generalized patterns from usage
* disable or limit memory where possible
* clear personal memory
* request deletion of account-linked memory
* understand when actions require confirmation

### 12.4 Trust Signals on Profiles

Each Guru should communicate:

* what it remembers
* how it learns
* whether it can make intros
* whether it can execute actions
* what external integrations it uses
* who created it
* how it is evaluated

### 12.5 Safety Modes

Certain categories may require stronger restrictions, disclaimers, or approval mechanics, especially if they involve:

* money movement
* token launches
* legal-sensitive guidance
* health-adjacent guidance
* relationship-sensitive intros

## 13. Wisdom Measurement Framework

Guruforge should define a multi-dimensional understanding of wisdom.

Wisdom is not the same as popularity, revenue, or verbosity.

A truly wise Guru should:

* help people effectively
* improve over time
* calibrate uncertainty well
* generalize useful patterns responsibly
* avoid overclaiming
* preserve trust
* create outcomes, not just content

### 13.1 Wisdom Score

The platform should compute a composite Wisdom Score.

Suggested dimensions:

* retention
* user satisfaction
* outcome quality
* calibration quality
* trust / safety quality
* shared pattern usefulness
* intro success quality
* contribution diversity and validation

### 13.2 Dimension Definitions

#### Retention

Signals ongoing value.
Examples:

* average subscription duration
* weekly or monthly active paying users
* repeat usage rates

#### Satisfaction

Signals perceived quality.
Examples:

* ratings
* post-session feedback
* refund rate
* complaint rate

#### Outcome Quality

Measures whether the Guru helped achieve meaningful results.
This should vary by category.

Examples:

* actions completed successfully
* proposals drafted
* docs improved
* intros accepted
* recurring problems resolved

#### Calibration Quality

Measures whether the Guru is appropriately confident, self-correcting, and grounded.

Examples:

* correction rate
* overclaim rate
* confidence alignment
* uncertainty handling quality

#### Trust / Safety Quality

Measures responsible behavior.
Examples:

* privacy violations prevented
* low complaint frequency
* safe action confirmation behavior
* low confusion around memory usage

#### Shared Pattern Usefulness

Measures whether the Guru’s generalized learning is actually helping future users.
Examples:

* pattern reuse success
* downstream outcome lift
* reuse across multiple users or contexts

#### Intro Success Quality

For connection-enabled Gurus.
Examples:

* relevant intros proposed
* intros accepted by both parties
* successful collaboration outcomes

### 13.3 Guru Development Score

Separate from wisdom, each Guru should have a Development Score reflecting maturity.

Possible factors:

* number of validated patterns
* corpus quality
* memory richness
* category specialization
* integration sophistication
* calibration maturity
* public profile completeness

## 14. Contributor Influence System

One of Guruforge’s most innovative features is the ability to recognize the users who improve a Guru.

### 14.1 Problem

Some users do more than consume. They teach, correct, challenge, refine, and provide edge cases that improve the Guru’s future performance.

The platform should try to identify and measure this.

### 14.2 Contribution Types

A user may contribute by:

* introducing a novel insight
* correcting a false assumption
* sharing a reusable resource
* demonstrating a better workflow
* helping validate a pattern
* revealing a useful edge case
* improving the Guru’s calibration
* helping generate successful introductions or connections

### 14.3 Contribution Score

Each user can have a contribution profile relative to a specific Guru.

Possible dimensions:

* novelty
* reusability
* downstream impact
* validation strength
* originality
* durability over time

### 14.4 Reward Logic

Contributions should not be rewarded simply because they exist.
They should only matter meaningfully when:

* they were generalized safely
* they improved future outcomes
* they survived validation
* they were not contradicted later

This creates a fairer and more robust system.

### 14.5 User Contribution Dashboard

Each contributor could see:

* top Gurus influenced
* types of contributions made
* validated patterns influenced
* influence over time
* pending contributions under evaluation
* status for future rewards or recognition

## 18. Guru Lifecycle

Every Guru should have a visible progression path.

### Stage 0: Draft

Configured but not published.

### Stage 1: Newborn Guru

Recently launched, low data, early users, no established wisdom score.

### Stage 2: Useful Specialist

Demonstrates basic quality, stable interactions, early retention and satisfaction.

### Stage 3: Trusted Guru

Shows strong retention, healthy calibration, reliable outcomes, and validated patterns.

### Stage 4: Compounding Guru

Clearly improves through usage, has strong shared pattern memory, rising influence, and meaningful network effects.

### Stage 5: Ecosystem Guru

Has public recognition, high trust, mature integrations, strong contributor network, and is eligible for advanced economic or onchain features.

This lifecycle should be visible in the product and motivate creators to improve quality rather than only chasing traffic.

## 19. Tokenization and Onchain Identity

Tokenization should be treated as a later-stage milestone.

### 19.1 Why Tokenize a Guru

A mature Guru may become more than a paid service. It may become:

* a public digital entity
* a community-aligned network
* a fee-generating agent brand
* a contributor-coordinated ecosystem
* an onchain-recognized reputation object

### 19.2 Eligibility Criteria

A Guru should only become eligible for tokenization if it reaches thresholds such as:

* sufficient active clients
* healthy retention over time
* strong wisdom score
* acceptable trust and safety score
* established contribution measurement
* clear creator identity and profile maturity

### 19.3 Token Utility Possibilities

Potential token roles may include:

* community identity
* alignment mechanism
* fee routing and rewards distribution
* contributor incentives
* access benefits
* governance of selected public parameters

### 19.4 Tokenization Principles

* tokenization should reflect real utility and trust
* it should not be the starting point for most Gurus
* it should not undermine product quality
* it should feel earned

### 19.5 Public Profile + Onchain Identity

Mature Gurus may have:

* public profile pages
* verifiable creator linkage
* onchain registration
* standard agent identity metadata
* public metrics snapshots

This can position Guruforge as a bridge between agent marketplaces and open agent identity ecosystems.

## 25. Key Risks

### 25.1 Privacy Leakage

The greatest risk is accidental leakage of private user details into generalized Guru responses or shared memory.

### 25.2 Fake Wisdom

A Guru may appear smart through style while actually producing shallow or unstable value.

### 25.3 Incentive Gaming

If contribution rewards are introduced too early, users may spam low-quality “insights” to farm status or tokens.

### 25.4 Overcomplicated Pricing

Dynamic pricing can become confusing or feel exploitative if not clearly governed.

### 25.5 Marketplace Cold Start

Without strong seed Gurus, the marketplace may feel empty or generic.

### 25.6 Token Distraction

Launching token mechanics too early may attract speculation before trust and usefulness are mature.

---

## 26. Strategic Recommendations

### 26.1 Start With a Strong Wedge

Do not launch with every possible Guru category.
Start with a focused domain where users already value ongoing expertise and action.

Strong starting options:

* founder / operator Gurus
* DeFi / crypto Gurus
* creator / audience Gurus
* research and wisdom aggregators

### 26.2 Make Memory Legible

Users should clearly understand:

* what the Guru remembers
* what stays private
* what is generalized
* how the Guru gets smarter

### 26.3 Show Development Publicly

A Guru’s growth journey should be visible.
This transforms the platform from static chatbot listings into a living intelligence ecosystem.

### 26.4 Delay Token Rewards Until Attribution Is Robust

Get contribution measurement right before tying it to financial incentives.

### 26.5 Build for Trust, Then Magic

The product can be ambitious, but trust must come before spectacle.

## 28. Final Product Thesis

Guruforge is a platform for creating and distributing Gurus: specialized AI agents that people can discover, subscribe to, and interact with in familiar environments like Telegram.

Unlike ordinary assistants, Gurus develop over time through layered memory, careful calibration, and reusable generalized learning. They can become more useful, more trusted, more connected, and more valuable as they serve more users.

The long-term opportunity is not only to build a marketplace of paid agents, but to create a system where digital expertise becomes measurable, improvable, economically aligned, and eventually recognized as its own kind of evolving entity.

That is the product.
That is the forge.
